leadership

phase zero

What is your experience of compassionate (or not) leadership?

My most recent boss was very understanding when I went through some health issues and needed an operation on my sinuses. He never once made me worry about the workload and organised cover for all of my work. He made sure I knew that health was the number one priority over work. In our one on one conversations he knew that I wasn't overly confident in my abilities and quite often he opened up about how he never used to be that confident and that it will come with time. It made me feel like he was on our side and truly wanted us to develop rather than only focused on the companies deadlines and his own agenda.

Do the multiplying goodness meditation. How did you find it?

I found it easy to visualise when you think of goodness as white light. It made me think that perhaps we too easily look for the negatives in situations rather than seeing the good that is everywhere around us. It felt quite heartwarming to visualise multiplying goodness.

Do the Tonglen meditation. How did you find it?

When i visualised myself I felt afterwards that my suffering wasnt so bad and I could see the bad energy coming out and the good energy going back in. I felt quite powerful and that perhaps doing something small like this could in fact have a huge impact on trying to take away some of the suffering for someone.

How does the SCARF model apply to your Phase 0 experience so far? How will it apply to bootcamp?

status

During phase zero, I haven't encountered much weight on status. If anything, it feels like noone sees themselves as having more importance over someone else. When asking questions on the slack helpdesk everyone is always so eager to help and noone ever makes you feel like you underneath them even though they may know more than you do.

During the bootcamp I think it will be important to only look at status in reference to yourself. It is easy to look at the teacher or the person who knows the least in the class and put them into a pecking order, but thinking in this way is not going to help anything. Focusing on "playing against yourself" will be beneficial as we will be constantly be learning new skills over the next nine weeks and to compare yourself week on week will activate many status rewards.

certainty

During phase zero, there have been many uncertain moments. Especially because you are working mainly on your own so you do start to feel uncertain as to whether you are doing it right, or keeping up with the work. That's why I think it was so important to keep the lines of communication open and attend the weekly check-ins, check in on Slack often or ask questions and communicate with everyone through Slack.

During bootcamp, there won't be as much uncertainty, yes we will be unsure of many things but at least we will be certain that we will have a strong network of people around to help us.

autonomy

Autonomy is not the stress itself that gets to you; it’s the feeling of helplessness in the face of that stress. During phase zero there were a lot of moments where there was stress and dealing with that stress was not always easy. At times my first reaction would be panic. Panic that I wasn't smart enough to be doing this, panic that I won't be able to keep up on the bootcamp, panic that everyone else was moving faster than I was. The key to dealing with the stress was to work through problems and realise that everyone is going through the same.

During bootcamp I think that feeling of helplessness will be greatly diminished by being part of the group and doing work in pairs with the support of the tutors.

relatedness

Relatedness is the perception of whether another person is a "friend" or "foe". During my experience so far in phase zero, I have never really felt like anyone was a "foe" to begin with. I think the culture of dev academy is definitely one that has instilled that everyone is a "friend" right from the get go. During the check ins, everyone has been so friendly.

During bootcamp I think this will continue and I get the feeling walking in on the first day will be much of the same. We are all in the same boat and have bonded (without even meeting each other yet) over the shared experience of phase zero.

fairness

I think during bootcamp this will be a key quality to exhibit towards others. I think people will be generous with their time at the expense of their own progress in order to help someone get up to speed. It's not fair to leave someone lagging feeling hopeless in order for you to fly way ahead.

Have a difficult conversation. How did it go? (It's a valuable skill, but can take practice to develop!)

I didn't have anything to difficult to discuss at the present. But I can recall the last difficult conversation I had and how it played out.

It was when I resigned from my prior job after I had accepted a place in dev academy. I knew it wasn't going to go down well as I had only been with the company for 7 months. I visualised how I thought the conversation would go, in my head i didn't see it ending well. It actually went better than expected, there was the initial shock but once he had got over that he was able to view it as a great opportunity and was really happy for me. In my head I assumed he would only be thinking about the consequences for him and having to rehire and retrain someone, but he was actually able to look past that.

Any other musings?

I found the mindful emailing topic hit quite close to home. Quite often I receive an email and immediately interpret ambiguous emails in a negative way. I know from experience that quite often if you just pick up the phone and talk to the person, you straight away feel at ease and things were never as bad as they seem.

In the future I will pick up the phone more often and try and avoid emailing. If I do have to send an email then I am going to take extra care and visualise myself on the receiving end reading it with negative bias.
